# Chapter 4: The Vertical Integration

> "They're playing both sides of every transaction. And they do it all under a tax exemption meant for charity."

---

## The Thesis

The largest health systems don't just provide care — they insure it. They set the price as the provider and pay the price as the payer. The patient is trapped in the middle, locked into a system that profits whether they're healthy or sick, whether claims are paid or denied.

---

## Section 1: The Double Play

- Health systems that own insurance plans
- How it works: employer contracts with system health plan, employees must use system facilities
- The incentive structure: every dollar kept is profit on both sides
- "Vertical integration" as euphemism for control

---

## Section 2: The Locked-In Patient

- Your employer's plan IS your hospital's plan
- Network design as captivity
- Narrow networks, limited choice
- The illusion of insurance competition

---

## Section 3: Denials from Within

- Your "nonprofit" hospital denying your claim
- System-owned plan denial rates vs independent insurers
- Prior authorization as profit center
- The patient caught between two arms of the same entity

## Section 4: Case Studies

### UPMC Health Plan
- The model for system-owned insurance
- Market share in Western Pennsylvania
- Highmark war: what happens when systems compete with insurers
- Now they ARE the insurer

### Kaiser Permanente
- Fully integrated, fully controlled
- The original HMO
- No exit: your doctor, your hospital, your insurer — all Kaiser
- Praised as efficient, experienced as captive

### Geisinger Health Plan
- Rural Pennsylvania dominance
- "Proven Health Navigator" model
- Integration as inevitability

### Intermountain Health
- SelectHealth insurance arm
- Utah/Idaho regional control
- "Best practices" or best monopoly?

### The Blues
- BCBS chapters with delivery system arms
- HCSC, BCBS Michigan, etc.
- Quasi-systems with insurance DNA
- The mutual/nonprofit distinction without a difference
