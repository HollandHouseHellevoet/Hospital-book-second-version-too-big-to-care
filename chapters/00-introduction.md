## Draft

### Holland

I was born in Baarn and raised in Hellevoetsluis.

Baarn sits inland, a quiet town in Utrecht province. Hellevoetsluis sits on the water in Zuid Holland — a former naval port, a town built around locks and a harbor, the salt wind coming in off the Haringvliet. A short drive from my front door was Rotterdam, the largest sea port in the world.

Every commodity humans trade moves through Rotterdam. Oil. Grain. Steel. Coffee. Cars. Electronics. Containers by the millions, every year, funneled through a single mouth on the North Sea. A child growing up in that part of the country grows up inside a functioning global marketplace whether he notices it or not.

The Dutch invented the modern market. Tulip mania, 1637 — the first recorded bubble. The Amsterdam Stock Exchange, 1602 — the first anywhere. The Dutch East India Company — the first joint-stock corporation. Price discovery, public trading, transparent quotation: all of it was built on a few square miles of reclaimed swamp.

I grew up inside that inheritance.

A bag of coffee had a price. A barrel of oil had a price. A tulip had a price. A ship in Rotterdam had a price. The price moved. Buyers and sellers could see it move. If you did not like the price, you walked to the next stall — or you sailed to the next port.

That is what a market is. Not a slogan. Not a theory. A place where price is visible and exit is available.

Every functioning economy on earth runs on that idea.

Except one.

### Guyana, 1996

In 1996 I went on a mission trip to Guyana.

We ran clinics in two economies. On the coast, in Georgetown, patients who could pay paid — a small fee, quoted before the visit, in Guyanese dollars. Patients who could not pay did not pay. The clinic ran either way.

Upriver, past the roads, past the power lines, past the phone signal, the money disappeared. In the jungle it was all volunteer. A physician, a stethoscope, a box of medications, and a line of people who had walked for hours to be there.

Care happened in both places. Nobody needed a $400,000 revenue cycle team to close the loop. Nobody argued with a prior authorization fax. Nobody got a surprise bill three months later from an out-of-network radiologist.

I flew home to the richest healthcare system on the planet and could not get a straight price on an X-ray.

That was the moment.

Healthcare in America has no market. No price discovery. No exchange. No visible quote. No exit. Just opacity, and inside the opacity, extraction.

A market without prices is not a market. It is a hunting ground.

### 2010 — HcX

In 2010 I went to work on a first-principles solution.

A commodities exchange for medical treatments and services. I called it the Healthcare Commodities Exchange. HcX.

The premise was simple. Corn trades on an exchange. Oil trades on an exchange. Coffee, cattle, copper, wheat, natural gas — every real commodity in the world trades on an open market where the price is visible and the contract is enforceable. A knee replacement is a commodity. A colonoscopy is a commodity. An MRI is a commodity. They can be standardized. They can be priced. They can be traded.

Build the exchange, and price discovery arrives overnight. Build the exchange, and the opacity dies. Build the exchange, and the country that hosts it owns the infrastructure of global medicine — the way Chicago owns grain, London owns metals, and New York owns equities.

The United States could make an enormous amount of money controlling the world's supply and demand of medical care. The United States chose not to.

I patented the mechanism. I trademarked the brand. I walked the thesis into rooms full of people who were supposed to want it.

Nobody was ready.

The hospitals did not want prices. The insurers did not want prices. The consultants did not want prices. The regulators did not want prices. The only people who wanted prices were patients and physicians, and neither of them had a seat at the table.

I learned the lesson every reformer eventually learns. The people who run this system are not confused. They are not waiting for a better idea. They built the opacity on purpose, and they will fight anyone who tries to lift it.

### Cambridge, 2012

HcX did not pay the bills.

An idea that early needs time, and time costs money. So I took consulting work. In 2012 that work took me to Cambridge, England, to a client named Toby Churchill.

Toby Churchill was a man and a company. The company built augmentative and alternative communication devices — machines that give a voice to people who have lost theirs. The man built them because he understood what they were for.

Stephen Hawking was Toby's friend. That is the clean way of saying that when the most famous physicist in the world needed a voice, the firm that made it was a small English company in Cambridge run by a person who cared about the work.

ALS steals speech before it steals breath. Motor neurons die. The body becomes a cage. What Toby built was a key — a tablet, a switch, a camera, a carefully tuned piece of software that let a mind find its way back into the room.

I flew from Heathrow to Phoenix Sky Harbor without uttering a word out loud. Every syllable of that journey — the boarding pass, the customs line, the cab, the hotel front desk — ran through the machine. It was one of the most beautiful experiences of my life.

That trip taught me something this industry tries very hard to hide. Medicine, at its best, is humane. The tools are beautiful. The people who build them are motivated. The patients are patient. The relationships are sacred.

The scam is not the medicine. The scam is the structure that has been built on top of it.

### Building Anyway

So I went and built.

Ambulatory surgery centers. Direct contracts between employers and physicians. A health plan. Three companies started, three companies exited. None of it was easy. All of it worked, because the minute you put a real price on a real procedure, patients and employers show up.

I run ROJAS MEDIA now. The Rojas Report goes out every day. The Physicians Edge records three nights a week. MedMerge builds infrastructure for independent physicians. PhyCap invests physician capital into physician companies — I am the only non-physician general partner at the fund, and I take that seriously.

I sit on the board of Physician Led Healthcare for America.

I have spent twenty years inside this industry. I have negotiated with the systems. I have sat across from the associations. I have watched the committees vote.

I know how the game is played because I have been at the table.

### Why This Book

The American healthcare system is not broken.

That word — *broken* — is the single most expensive lie in the national conversation. Broken implies accident. Broken implies good intentions gone wrong. Broken implies a fixable bug.

This system is not broken. This system is rigged.

The top health systems consolidated into regional monopolies. They vertically integrated into insurance so they could set the price as hospital and pay the price as payer. They captured their trade associations and turned them into lobbying arms. They funded the committees that write the laws. They rotated their staffers through the agencies that enforce the laws. They extracted wealth — real, measurable, tax-exempt wealth — under the banner of nonprofit mission and academic prestige.

The payers copied the playbook. The systems built it.

That is the argument of this book. Over the next twelve chapters I will name the systems, name the associations, name the members of Congress, and name the revolving-door operators who move between them. Every claim has a receipt. Every number has a source. Every villain has a first and last name.

This is not a screed. It is an indictment.

### Who This Book Is For

If you are a patient who has stared at a hospital bill and felt the room tilt — this book is for you.

If you are a physician who trained for a decade to practice medicine and now spends half your day fighting prior auth denials — this book is for you.

If you are an employer writing seven-figure checks for health benefits and watching your people get worse care each year — this book is for you.

If you are a policymaker who wants to do something real and is tired of being handed talking points by the people you are supposed to regulate — this book is for you.

If you work for a health system, a trade association, or a PAC named in these pages — this book is also for you. You already know most of what is in here. You know because you helped build it.

### The Road Ahead

Part I names the systems. Nonprofit, academic, for-profit, and the vertically integrated hybrids. The structural layer.

Part II names the associations. AHA, FAH, AHIP. The lobbying layer.

Part III names the enablers. The committees, the members, the revolving door. The political layer.

Part IV is the reckoning. The cracks that are already showing. The prescription — what Congress will not do, what the market must build, and what you can do starting today.

I did not write this book to vent. I wrote it to put a map in your hands.

The system was built. That means it can be unbuilt.

Let's go.
